I've written four languages seriously. Not one of them because it was trending.

Every switch happened the same way. I'd be sitting with a problem the current tool couldn't solve cleanly, and I'd feel a language on the other side of that problem pulling at me. So I'd cross over, pay the tax of being a beginner again, and keep going. My stack isn't a shelf of things I collected. It's a trail of problems I chased.

## Scratch, where it was still play

The first one didn't feel like programming at all.

Scratch, when I was young, was just play. Drag a block, watch a cat move, drag another. There was no syntax to fear because there was no syntax. But underneath the cartoon blocks was the same thing I'd spend the next decade doing: this happens, then that happens, and if this, then something else. I was writing logic and calling it a game.

I don't count Scratch as a language I know. I count it as the place I learned that a program is just a sequence of decisions, small enough that a kid can hold them. Everything after was that same idea wearing heavier clothes.

## C#, and knowing when to stop

C# was the first switch I made on purpose. I wanted to build games, not play them, and the engine spoke C#, so I learned C#.

Here's the honest part: I never went deep. I learned enough to make things move, to wire a scene together, to feel the loop of build and run and fix. Then I stopped. Not because I failed at it, but because I found the door I was actually looking for, and it wasn't game development.

I used to think shallow knowledge was a kind of debt. It isn't, not always. C# taught me the shape of a typed, compiled, object-heavy language, and that shape stayed with me even after I put the language down. You don't have to master a tool for it to change how you think. Sometimes you just need to hold it long enough to learn what it's trying to teach, then set it down and move.

## Python, the one I stopped leaving

Then came Python, and for the first time I stopped switching.

Python didn't feel like a tool I was using so much as one that kept saying yes. I needed a backend, and it handed me Django, then Django Ninja when I wanted the API to be leaner. I needed to serve models, and FastAPI was right there, fast enough to sit at the front of an MLOps pipeline without complaint. I wanted to actually build with machine learning, and the whole shelf opened up: scikit-learn for the classic problems, YOLO when I needed to see, PyTorch underneath it all.

I'll be honest about PyTorch too. I'm not deep in it. I can build and train and get a model to behave, but I'm not writing custom kernels or reinventing architectures. I know where my floor is.

Where I did go deep was agents. LangChain, then LangGraph when a chain wasn't enough and I needed a real graph of state and decisions. Agno and the rest of that world. This is where my systems instinct finally met AI, because an agent isn't a prompt, it's a machine of interacting parts, and that's the thing I've loved building since I was moving pistons around. Postgres held the state underneath all of it, because state that isn't stored honestly turns on you eventually.

Python is where I settled, not because it was the best at any one thing, but because it never made me leave to get to the next thing.

## Go, the newest pull

Now something is pulling again.

The backend I built in Python works, but I can feel where it's slow, and slow stopped being abstract the moment it mattered. So I'm learning Go, writing it against Gin, and I'm learning it the only way that's ever worked for me: by shipping a real project with it, not by drilling tutorials.

I'm a beginner in Go right now, and I'm saying that on purpose. There's a specific discomfort in being bad at something again after years of being fluent, and I've learned to read that discomfort as a good sign. It means the problem was real enough to pull me across another line. The friction is the curriculum. It always has been.

## So

If you lined up my stack, it might look like a list of trends I chased. It isn't. It's a record of problems that were bigger than the tool I had when I met them.

Scratch taught me logic is just decisions. C# taught me you can learn a tool's lesson without marrying it. Python taught me what it feels like when a language stops getting in your way. Go is teaching me, right now, that being a beginner is the price of staying honest about what the work actually needs.

I don't think of myself as a Python engineer, or a Go engineer, or any language's engineer. The language was never the point. The problem was. I just keep following it, and I pick up whatever tool it hands me on the way up.
