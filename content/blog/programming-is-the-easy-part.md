The wall I hit wasn't the code. It was a character I couldn't draw.

I had a small thing moving on the screen. It walked when I pressed a key, stopped when I let go, and I'd written every line of that myself. Then I looked at it for a while and realized the problem. It moved, but it wasn't alive. Two frames of animation, a body that slid across the ground like a sticker being dragged. Technically it was working. It was also dead, and I couldn't tell you why, and I definitely couldn't fix it.

That was the moment I understood I'd walked into game development through the only door I knew how to open, and mistaken it for the whole building.

## I came in through the part I could handle

I started because I wanted to make something, not just play it. I picked Unity, and I told myself the honest first goal was to get better at C# along the way. That part felt like home. A typed, compiled language, an engine with a clear model underneath it, a build-and-run loop I already loved. I had a story in my head too, not shaped yet, just a feeling of a world and a mood I wanted to sit inside.

For a while it went the way I expected. I wired up input. I got a scene to hold together. I made the thing move. Every problem in that stretch was a problem I knew how to attack, because it was the same kind of problem I'd been attacking for years: break it into pieces, isolate the one that's wrong, fix it, try again. Code is a system, and systems are the thing I take apart for fun.

So I thought I was most of the way there. I had the hard part working, or what I assumed was the hard part. I was wrong about which part that was.

## The wall had a face

The wall was character design, and it had a face I couldn't draw.

I stepped out of the code and into the question of who this character actually was, what they looked like, how they should feel to see on the screen, and I had nothing. Not a blank I could fill by working harder. A blank I didn't know how to fill at all. In code, when I don't know something, I know the shape of the not-knowing. I know what to search, what to read, which piece to poke. Here I didn't even have that. I couldn't name what was missing.

I tried pixel art because it looked, from the outside, like the approachable end. It isn't. Everything I made stayed stuck at four-bit. Not a style, just bad. Flat where it should have had depth, muddy where it should have read clean, wrong in ways I could see plainly and not describe. I could tell it was bad. That was the cruelest part. I had enough taste to know it wasn't good and nowhere near enough skill to close the gap between the two.

Then the animation, and the dead little character sliding across the floor. Two frames. I learned, staring at it, that the difference between a thing that moves and a thing that feels alive is not a line of code. It's a hundred tiny decisions about weight and timing and anticipation that an animator holds in their hands the way I hold a call stack in my head. I didn't have those hands. I'd never trained them.

## And behind it, more walls

What broke me a little was that it didn't stop.

Character design wasn't one wall. It was the first wall, and behind it stood another, and behind that another. I'd get a foothold on the art and then remember the levels had to be designed, that the space a player moves through is its own craft with its own century of hard-won knowledge, pacing and readability and the quiet art of teaching someone how to play without a single line of tutorial text. Behind that, more. The world needed a mood, a direction, a coherence that no single asset carries on its own.

I never even reached the soundtrack. I want to be honest about that, because it's the detail that tells the truth. I ran out of climb before I got to a discipline that, on its own, people spend their whole lives inside. Sound was a mountain I could see in the distance and never set foot on, because I was still stuck at the base of the first one.

Every direction I looked, there was another field with its own depth, its own masters, its own decades. And I had walked in holding the one skill I was good at, thinking it was the currency the whole place ran on.

## The system that wouldn't come apart

Here's the thing I keep circling back to, because it's the part that actually changed how I think.

Everything else I've ever built, I've cracked the same way. I took Linux apart until the operating system stopped being a wall and became something I could read top to bottom. I learned shell scripting one annoyance at a time. I followed problems into four languages, paying the beginner's tax each time, because I trusted that any system yields if you're willing to disassemble it and understand every piece. That trust has never once let me down. Take it apart, see how it fits, own it. That's the whole method, and it works on everything.

It didn't work on this.

Art and level design and character design were not systems I could take apart to understand. Or maybe they are, to the people who live in them, but from where I stood they behaved like a different kind of thing entirely. You can't read taste line by line like a config file. There's no flag I could flip to make the sprite feel alive, no piece I could isolate to find the bug in a character that had no soul. The knowledge wasn't structured the way I know how to attack knowledge. It lived in thousands of hours of hand and eye, in a sense that gets built by doing, not by reasoning. My systems brain reached for the seams and found a surface with no seams in it.

That was genuinely disorienting. I've built my whole way of working on the belief that understanding is a thing you can force if you're stubborn enough. And here was a room full of things that don't give themselves up to stubbornness. They give themselves up to years. To practice I hadn't done and couldn't shortcut. I met the edge of my own method, and the method just stopped.

## So

I came in thinking the code was the mountain. The code was the foothill.

I set the project down. I won't pretend that felt good, because it didn't. It was disappointment, plain and real, the specific sting of someone who's used to being able to figure things out running into something he couldn't. But it wasn't the disappointment of quitting. It was the disappointment of finally seeing how big the real thing is, and knowing I hadn't earned it yet.

Because that's what actually happened. I didn't fail to make a game. I found out what making one costs, and the number was honest and much larger than I'd assumed, and most of it wasn't priced in code. The programming, the part I was proud of, the part I thought I was bringing to the table, turned out to be the cheap seat. The expensive part was everything I'd never trained a single day for.

So the game went where it had to go, onto the list. Not the trash, the list. Real life had opinions about my time, the work and the job and the years that don't pause so you can chase a hobby down a hole. It became the one-day project, the thing I'll come back to as mine, on my own hours, and finally publish. And when I say publish I mean small and finished, not big and imagined. One game. Modest. Done. That's the whole ambition.

I'm still standing at the bottom of the real climb, and I want to be clear that I haven't started up it yet. The honest version of this story doesn't end with me conquering anything. It ends with me knowing, for the first time, exactly what the mountain is. Which is not nothing. Every hard thing I've ever done started the same way, with me at the base of it, finally seeing it for its real size.

I'll come back for this one. Not because I'm good at it. Because I'm not, and that's the oldest signal I have that something's worth the climb.
