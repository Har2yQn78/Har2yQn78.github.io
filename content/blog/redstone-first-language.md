The first thing I ever built that actually worked was a door.

Not a real door. A hidden one in Minecraft, made of redstone, that slid open when I flipped a lever across the room. It took me an entire afternoon. I placed dust, watched it do nothing, tore it out, placed it again slightly differently, and repeated that loop until the wall finally moved. I didn't know it then, but that loop, build, break, adjust, try again, was the most important thing a game ever taught me.

I became a software engineer years later. Looking back, the path runs straight through that door.

## Learning without realizing

Games never sat me down and explained problem solving. They just refused to let me win until I figured it out.

A big contraption was never one problem. It was twenty small ones stacked together: a clock here, a piston sequence there, a timing issue I had to isolate before the whole thing would fire. I learned to break a thing I couldn't solve into pieces I could, not because someone told me to, but because it was the only way forward.

Most of those pieces failed first. Repeatedly. Failure wasn't a setback, it was just information. When the door opened halfway and jammed, the game wasn't punishing me. It was telling me exactly where my logic was wrong. So I'd trace the signal back, find the spot where it died, and fix it.

I was debugging. I just didn't have the word for it yet.

## Consume to create

At some point, playing stopped being enough.

I had spent years living inside other people's systems, and I started wanting to build my own. Not a clever trick inside someone else's game, but the game itself. That shift, from consuming to creating, is the moment everything turned.

My first real language was C#, written against a game engine. It should have felt foreign. It didn't. Variables were just levers and signals with names. A function was a contraption I could trigger from somewhere else. State was the thing I had already spent years fighting, except now I could finally name it.

Then came Python, and the same pattern held. The syntax was new. The thinking wasn't. Programming didn't feel like learning a new skill so much as finally getting words for one I already had.

That was the click.

## Systems all the way down

The longer I built, the more one lesson kept repeating: nothing exists in isolation.

In a game, every system touches another. Change the timing on one component and something three steps away breaks in a way you didn't predict. You learn to hold the whole machine in your head, to respect that a small change can ripple, and to design pieces that snap together instead of tangling.

That is most of software engineering. Modules that interact. State that has to be managed honestly or it turns on you. Performance that stops being abstract the moment your build stutters. Constraints that force you to get creative inside a box instead of wishing the box away.

Games built the temperament for it too. Curiosity to poke at how something works. Patience to sit with a problem that doesn't move for hours. Persistence to try again after the tenth failed attempt, because the eleventh might be the one. None of that is a technical skill. All of it is the job.

## Where it shows up now

These days the contraptions are different, but the instinct is the same.

When I work on AI systems, I'm still breaking a fuzzy problem into parts small enough to reason about. When I design backend architecture, I'm holding a machine of interacting pieces in my head and watching for the ripple. When I tear my Linux setup apart for the hundredth time, or build a small tool to kill one daily annoyance, it's the same afternoon loop from years ago: build, break, adjust, try again.

I don't think in isolated features. I think in systems, because the first systems I ever loved were the ones I had to coax into working one block at a time.

## So

I didn't become a developer because I played games.

I became a developer because certain games taught me to love solving problems.

Looking back, Redstone may have been my first programming language. It just didn't use text.
